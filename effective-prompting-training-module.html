What Is Prompting?

A prompt is simply what you type when you talk to an AI. Understanding what makes a prompt effective is the first step to getting dramatically better results — and it's simpler than you think.

A prompt is any text you send to an AI model — a question, an instruction, a piece of text to analyze, or a combination of all three. The AI reads your prompt and generates the most useful response it can based on exactly what you wrote.

Think of it like giving directions to someone who has never been to your office before. The clearer and more specific your instructions, the better your chance of them arriving at the right place.

💡
Examples of prompts: "Summarize this email in 3 bullets." / "Write a follow-up message to a candidate we're rejecting." / "What are the pros and cons of switching to a quarterly review cycle?"

AI models are very literal. They respond to what you actually typed, not what you meant to type. If your prompt is ambiguous, the AI will make its best guess — which is often not what you wanted. This is why wording matters.

The AI also has no memory of past conversations by default. Each time you start a new session, it starts fresh with no context about you, your role, or your work — unless you tell it.

✅
Try it now: Ask Claude "Tell me about Pelago" versus "Give me a 3-bullet summary of what a healthcare addiction treatment company does, written for a new employee." Notice how much more useful the second response is.

The best prompts tend to include four elements. You don't need all four every time — but knowing they exist helps you diagnose why a prompt isn't working.

  1. Task — What do you want the AI to do? (Write, summarize, list, explain, compare…)
  2. Context — Who are you? Who is the audience? What's the situation?
  3. Format — How should the output look? (Bullet list, table, paragraph, email…)
  4. Constraints — Any limits? (Length, tone, things to avoid…)
💡
Example using all four: "Write a 3-sentence summary [format + constraint] of the following meeting notes [context] for a non-clinical audience who wasn't present [constraint]: [paste notes here] [task]."
2

Core Techniques

These four techniques will immediately improve most of your AI interactions. Apply even one and you'll notice the difference in the quality of responses you get.

Vague prompts produce generic responses. The more specific you are about what you want, who it's for, and what "good" looks like, the better the output will be. Specificity is the single highest-leverage change you can make.

⚠️
Vague: "Write an email about the new policy."
✅
Specific: "Write a friendly 3-paragraph email to all Pelago employees announcing that we're switching to a new PTO tracking system starting June 1. The tone should be warm and encouraging, and end with a clear call to action to log in and update their remaining balance."

AI knows nothing about your specific situation unless you tell it. Sharing relevant context — who you are, what you're working on, who the audience is — dramatically improves results. You don't need to write paragraphs; even one extra sentence helps.

💡
Adding "I'm writing this for our clinical team who isn't familiar with Salesforce terminology" can completely transform the response — even if nothing else in the prompt changes.
✅
Useful context to add: Your role, the audience's role or background, the purpose of the document, any relevant constraints ("this will go to external partners"), or what you've already tried.

If you want a bullet list, say so. If you want a table, ask for one. If you want a short response, specify the length. AI will match whatever format you describe — but it won't guess if you don't ask.

✅
Format phrases to try: "In 3 bullet points…" / "As a table with columns for [X] and [Y]…" / "In 2 short paragraphs…" / "As a numbered checklist…" / "In plain English, no jargon…" / "Under 100 words…"

Telling the AI to "act as" a certain type of expert helps it calibrate vocabulary, tone, and depth. This is especially useful when you need professional or technical content, or when you want the AI to take a particular perspective.

💡
Try these role openers: "Act as an experienced HR professional and help me write…" / "You are a plain-English explainer. Summarize this so a new hire can understand it." / "Respond as a skeptical executive who needs to be convinced."

Advanced Moves

Once you have the basics, these three techniques help you unlock more nuanced, powerful outputs — especially for complex tasks or when your first attempt doesn't quite land.

You rarely need to get it perfect on the first try. Think of prompting as a conversation — if the first response isn't right, follow up with a refinement in the same chat. The AI will adjust based on what you say.

✅
Refinement phrases that work well: "That's close, but make it shorter and less formal." / "Good structure — now focus more on the patient perspective." / "Rewrite the second paragraph so it sounds less corporate." / "Add a line acknowledging that this is a difficult transition."
⚠️
Don't start a new chat every time something's off. Staying in the same conversation gives the AI context to improve — starting fresh loses that context.

For complex tasks, break them into steps and ask the AI to work through them one at a time. You can also ask the AI to "think step by step" before giving an answer — this tends to produce more accurate and thoughtful responses on anything analytical.

💡
Example: "First, list the three main risks in this proposal. Then, for each risk, suggest one mitigation strategy. Finally, write a one-paragraph executive summary of your findings." This forces structure into the AI's reasoning.
✅
The phrase "Think step by step before answering" is particularly effective for math, logic, comparisons, and any prompt where reasoning quality matters.

One of the most powerful prompting techniques is showing the AI an example of the output you want before asking it to produce more. This is called "few-shot" prompting, and it works because the AI matches the style, format, and tone of your example exactly.

💡
Template: "Here's the style I want: [paste your example]. Now write three more in the same style about [your topic]."
✅
This is especially useful for: internal communications with a specific voice, slide titles that match an existing deck's tone, job descriptions that mirror your existing ones, or any content where "sounding like us" matters.

Applying the specificity and format techniques from Chapter 2 to a real communication task.

How to do it

  1. Open claude.ai in a new tab.
  2. Think of a real email you need to send — or invent one (e.g., announcing a team update, following up after a meeting, declining a vendor).
  3. Use the prompt below as your starting point, filling in the brackets.

Starter prompt

Write a [warm/formal/direct] email to [audience] about [topic]. Keep it under [X] sentences, use plain language, and close with a clear call to action.
✅
Tip: Try changing the tone word (warm vs. formal vs. direct) and see how dramatically the output shifts with just one word changed.

What you'll practice

Using context and format instructions to turn a long document into an audience-appropriate summary.

How to do it

  1. Find some text to summarize — meeting notes, a policy doc, a long email thread, or a Slack conversation.
  2. Copy the text and paste it into Claude at the end of your prompt.
  3. Use the prompt below, then try changing the audience and see how the summary shifts.

Starter prompt

Summarize the following text in 3 bullet points for someone who wasn't in the meeting. Focus on decisions made and next steps. Use plain language and avoid acronyms: [paste your text here]
✅
Tip: Add "for a [clinical / non-technical / executive] audience" and notice how the vocabulary and emphasis change.

What you'll practice

Using a role assignment and specific constraints to generate more useful, creative output than a vague "give me ideas" prompt ever would.

How to do it

  1. Pick a real problem or challenge you're working on — a project you need to move forward, a process that needs improvement, or a decision you're weighing.
  2. Use the prompt below, filling in your actual challenge.
  3. Once you get the 5 ideas, follow up: "Rank these by feasibility and explain your reasoning."

Starter prompt

Act as a creative strategist with experience in healthcare operations. Give me 5 unconventional ideas for [your challenge]. For each idea, include one potential risk and one reason it could actually work at a company like Pelago.
✅
Tip: Change "Act as a creative strategist" to "Act as a skeptical CFO" or "Act as a frontline employee" to get completely different perspectives on the same problem.

What you'll practice

Using a structured, multi-part prompt to extract and format information from messy, real-world input.

How to do it

  1. Copy some meeting notes — real ones from a recent meeting, or quickly make up a short fake meeting scenario.
  2. Paste them at the end of the prompt below.
  3. After you get the table, follow up: "Now draft a brief follow-up email summarizing these action items for the team."

Starter prompt

Extract all action items from these meeting notes. For each action item, identify: (1) who owns it, (2) what specifically needs to be done, and (3) any deadline mentioned. Format your answer as a table with columns: Owner | Action | Deadline. If no deadline is mentioned, write "TBD": [paste your notes here]
✅
Tip: The follow-up prompt ("Now draft a follow-up email…") shows how staying in the same conversation lets you build on previous output without re-explaining everything.
